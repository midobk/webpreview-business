# Copywriting

Copy is design material. A distinctive layout filled with "Welcome to X, your trusted partner"
reads as templated anyway — and generic copy is the single most reliable tell of a
cheap-model page. These formulas make specificity the default.

## The "best regular" principle

Write every line as if the business's most loyal customer were describing it to a friend —
someone who knows the place, names real things, and doesn't sell. That voice is specific,
warm, and short. When a line sounds like a brochure, it's failing this test.

**The portability test (run on every headline and lede):** could this sentence be moved to a
competitor — or to a different industry — unchanged? If yes, it says nothing. Fix it by
inserting one of the fact sheet's five concrete nouns.

## Headline formulas

The `h1` is ≤ 9 words, contains at least one concrete noun from the fact sheet, and never
contains "welcome," "quality," "premium," or the business's category said generically
("your local plumber"). Six shapes that work:

1. **Craft noun + promise.** "Sharp fades, zero wait." / "Torque-spec work, straight answers."
2. **The [place] [thing] for [occasion].** "Cornwall's kitchen for long Sunday lunches."
3. **Imperative + concrete object.** "Book a chair. Leave sharper."
4. **Fragment pair** (nouns only, let them resonate): "Wood fire. Slow dough."
5. **Outcome with an edge** (numbers only if real): "Winter-ready in one visit."
6. **The quiet statement** (Porcelain/Midnight registers): "the room to yourself."

Draft three candidates, keep the one with the most specific noun. When producing several
sites in one batch, rotate formulas — the fragment pair is the strongest and becomes a tic
fastest; if the last site opened with one, this site doesn't.

**The "how did they know?" detail.** Every page carries at least one line only an insider
could write — the tell that someone has actually stood in the place: "the door is the one
with the eucalyptus wreath", "la cabine est au fond à gauche", "sell out by Sunday, usually
before the espresso machine cools". Derive it from the brief's concrete facts (place, hours,
layout, rhythm of the week); invent texture, never claims. One is enough — it does more
convincing than any adjective on the page.

## Ledes

One or two sentences under the headline: **place + one named service + one sensory or
practical detail.** "Cuts, color, and balayage in downtown Cornwall — book online and the
chair's ready when you are." Never restate the headline; add the next most useful fact.

## Section headings

Eyebrow = the plain category label ("Services", "The menu", "How it works") — navigation, not
poetry. The `h2` = the specific or benefit take, with the accent `<em>` on the words that
carry it: "Cuts, color, and *slow Saturdays*". Plain eyebrow + specific heading is the pair;
two clever lines stacked cancel out.

## Service cards

Name: the real service, 2–3 words ("Seasonal tires", not "Tire Solutions"). Description: 12–18
words, starting with a verb or concrete noun, stating what happens and the outcome — "Mount,
balance, and store your winters so spring changeover takes twenty minutes." Price only if the
brief supplies one.

## CTA labels

Every CTA says what happens when clicked, in the user's words:

| Page job | Primary CTA | Secondary |
|---|---|---|
| Book | "Book a chair" / "Reserve a table" | "See the menu" / "View services" |
| Call | "Call the shop" | "Get directions" |
| Quote | "Get a quote today" | "See recent work" |
| Visit | "Plan your visit" | "See hours" |
| Sign up | "Start free" | "See how it works" |

Banned on buttons: "Learn More", "Submit", "Click Here", "Get Started" (alone). A CTA keeps
the same wording everywhere it repeats on the page.

## About / story

90–120 words: one founder or origin fact (only if supplied) → what they do every morning
(method, materials, routine) → the place. Write "what we do," not "what we believe in."
A pull-quote in display type can carry the best sentence.

## Numbers & proof — the honesty rules

- Use numbers, reviews, awards, years **only if the brief supplies them.** Never approximate,
  never "hundreds of happy customers."
- With no proof available, write **process proof**: how working together goes (01 call, 02
  quote, 03 done), true guarantees from the brief, hours, service area. Concrete process reads
  as trustworthy; invented social proof reads as fake even when the visitor can't check it —
  and is an automatic reject in most client pipelines.
- Prices: real or absent. An honest menu without prices beats a fictional one with them.
- **Quotation marks are claims.** Putting a line in « » or " " with a name under it asserts
  that person said those words. Write the best sentence yourself and let the page own it —
  set it as a pull-quote in display type *without* quote marks or attribution — unless the
  brief supplies an actual quote. A fabricated founder line is a fabricated testimonial.
- At the gate you'll walk the **facts ledger**: every digit and every attribution on the page
  traced back to the fact sheet. Write copy expecting that audit — a plausible invented
  specific ("+2000 références") will be deleted there anyway, so spend the words on specifics
  the brief can back.

## Microcopy

- Hours: real, or "Call to confirm seasonal hours" — never invented.
- Forms: label what happens after ("We reply the same business day" — only if true/supplied).
  A demo form says so ("Demo preview — this form is disabled").
- Footer small print: © year + business name; demo notice if applicable; no invented legal
  links.

## Banned list

Reject any draft containing (case-insensitive): *welcome to · your one-stop shop · look no
further · nestled (in the heart of) · hidden gem · committed to excellence · exceed(ing) your
expectations · state-of-the-art · top-notch · unparalleled · we pride ourselves ·
your satisfaction is our (priority|guarantee) · solutions for all your … needs · elevate your ·
unleash · seamless experience · passion for (excellence|quality) · quality you can trust ·
best-in-class · dedicated professionals · vibrant · furthermore · in today's fast-paced world.*

These aren't merely clichés — each one is a sentence that could describe any business, which
means it describes none. The fix is never a synonym; it's a fact.

## Register per direction (one line each)

- **Heritage Wine:** warm and assured; said across a counter.
- **Fieldstone:** short declaratives; numbers over adjectives; no exclamation points.
- **Porcelain:** quiet second person; lowercase comfort; no superlatives.
- **Midnight Counter:** fewest words on the page; menu-card cadence.
- **Blueprint:** present-tense spec precision; every claim measurable.
- **Market Fresh:** first-name friendly; sensory nouns; time-of-day details.
- **Ledger:** verbs, capacities, tolerances; reads like a manifest.
- **Tidewater:** reassuring with edges — promises that include a time or a place.
